## Part 1: What You Have Going For You

### 1.1 The Regulatory Tailwind Is Exceptional

Three concurrent FASB mandates create genuine urgency:

**DISE (ASU 2024-03)** — Effective for fiscal years beginning after December 15, 2026. This means calendar-year Fortune 500 companies must comply starting with their **2027 annual financial statements**, but they need systems and processes ready by January 2027 at the latest. That means **the buying window is right now, through Q3/Q4 2026**. Early adoption is permitted, adding further urgency for companies that want to get ahead. Every single public business entity is subject to this. The FASB has acknowledged that many companies' financial reporting systems don't currently track information at the level of detail required, meaning virtually every Fortune 500 company needs to do *something*.

**Income Tax Reconciliation (ASU 2023-09)** — Already effective for PBEs for fiscal years beginning after December 15, 2024. Companies are already in their first year of compliance and many are struggling with the rate reconciliation disaggregation requirements (the 5% threshold for reconciling items). This is a "pain they're feeling right now" sell.

**Lease Disclosures (ASC 842)** — Already in effect and an ongoing operational headache. Companies have largely implemented the standard but many are still wrestling with the ongoing disclosure requirements, especially around lease modifications, reassessments, and the volume of required quantitative and qualitative disclosures.

### 1.2 Max Robason's KPMG Position

This is your single most valuable asset. As a director of sales at KPMG US, Max brings:

- **Warm introductions** to CFOs, controllers, and CAOs at companies KPMG audits or advises
- **Credibility by association** — "formerly of KPMG" carries enormous weight with Fortune 500 finance teams
- **Deep knowledge of the pain points** — he's seen firsthand what companies pay ($800K–$1.2M) and what they struggle with
- **Understanding of procurement processes** at large enterprises
- **Potentially, knowledge of which companies are most behind** on DISE readiness

**Critical question:** When does Max leave KPMG? Non-compete and non-solicitation clauses could significantly constrain his ability to sell to KPMG's existing clients for 12–24 months. This must be legally vetted. If he's restricted from contacting KPMG clients, the value of his rolodex drops substantially in the near term.

### 1.3 Price Disruption

Charging $150K–$300K versus KPMG's $800K–$1.2M is a **70–85% cost reduction**. This is a compelling value proposition, but it cuts both ways:

- **Pro:** CFOs love saving $500K–$900K on a compliance mandate they can't avoid
- **Con:** At Fortune 500 companies, "too cheap" triggers suspicion. A $150K vendor for a critical financial reporting function feels risky to a CFO whose signature is on the 10-K. You may need to price at the higher end ($250K–$300K) to be taken seriously, and even then some companies will choose the $800K KPMG option because nobody gets fired for hiring KPMG.

### 1.4 Tech Readiness

Your repo shows significant development already in place: EDGAR prospecting tools, DISE mapping engines, compliance dashboards, GL Intelligence platform, agent-based mapping systems, and approval workflows. This is a meaningful head start. The fact that you can build fast and cheap is a genuine advantage — but it's not the bottleneck.

## Part 2: What's Working Against You

### 2.1 The Sales Cycle Problem (This Is the Killer)

Enterprise sales to Fortune 500 companies for compliance software touching financial reporting typically take **6–12+ months** from first conversation to signed contract. Here's why:

- **6–10 stakeholders** on average for deals in this range; deals over $500K often involve 12–15 people
- **CFO final approval** is required in ~79% of enterprise deals
- **Security and compliance reviews** (SOC 2, vendor risk assessments) add 2–4 weeks minimum
- **70% of enterprise deals require a pilot or proof of concept**, adding 4–8 weeks
- **Legal review** of contracts at Fortune 500 companies typically takes 2–6 weeks
- **Budget cycle alignment** — many companies set budgets in Q4 for the following year. If the budget for this wasn't allocated in Q4 2025, it may need to come from discretionary funds, which requires higher-level approval

Starting in April 2026 with a 9-month runway, the math is brutal:

| Month | Activity | Realistic Output |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Apr–May | Tech finalization, GTM strategy, initial outreach, Max transition planning | 0 deals |
| Jun–Jul | First demos, discovery calls, POC discussions | 0 deals, 5–15 active opportunities |
| Aug–Sep | POCs underway, procurement processes initiated | 0–2 deals if you're lucky |
| Oct–Nov | First wave of closes, second wave in procurement | 2–5 cumulative deals |
| Dec | Year-end push, some Q1 budget unlocks | 3–8 cumulative deals |

**Best case with heroic execution: 5–8 closed deals by December 31, 2026.**

### 2.2 You Have No Brand

Truffles AI has $300K in revenue from different products and verticals. In the world of Fortune 500 financial reporting:

- You have zero case studies in this space
- You have zero SOC 2 compliance (presumably)
- You have no track record of handling SEC-critical disclosure data
- The name "Truffles AI" doesn't convey "we protect your 10-K"

Every Fortune 500 CFO considering you will ask: "Who else uses this?" And the answer for the first 6 months will be "nobody." This is the classic cold-start problem and it's especially severe in compliance where the downside of choosing wrong is restatements, SEC scrutiny, and personal liability.

### 2.3 The Competitive Landscape Is Real

- **Workiva** — $885M revenue, dominant in SEC disclosure management, already building DISE capabilities. They're the incumbent at hundreds of Fortune 500 companies.
- **BlackLine** — Leader in financial close and reconciliation
- **FloQast** — 2,800+ customers, strong mid-market presence, moving upmarket
- **Trintech (Adra)** — Financial close automation
- **Big 4 consulting arms** — Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG themselves will all be offering DISE implementation services

You're not just competing on price — you're asking companies to trust a 3-person startup with some of their most sensitive financial data during a first-time compliance mandate.

### 2.4 Three-Person Team Limitations

Even with Max selling full-time and you building full-time, you'll hit capacity constraints:

- Max can realistically manage 15–25 active sales conversations simultaneously
- Each POC/implementation will require your time, pulling you from product development
- Customer support for live clients will begin consuming bandwidth by Q3/Q4
- You'll need to handle legal, contracts, security questionnaires, and vendor risk assessments

## Part 4: What It Would Take to Maximize Outcomes

### The "Get to $2M" Playbook

**Month 1–2 (April–May 2026)**

1. **Legal clarity on Max's transition** — Non-compete, non-solicit, IP assignment, timeline
2. **SOC 2 Type I engagement** — Start immediately. Many Fortune 500 procurement teams won't engage without it. Get a Type I done within 90 days using an accelerated provider (Vanta, Drata, etc.)
3. **Build 3 demo environments** — One for each mandate (DISE, Tax Reconciliation, Lease)
4. **Create a prospect hit list** — Your EDGAR SAP ECC prospector is smart. Refine it to identify the 50 companies most likely to buy: SAP ECC users (hardest time disaggregating data), non-Big-4 audit clients (less loyalty to incumbents), companies in industries with complex expense structures (manufacturing, energy, pharma)
5. **Pricing strategy** — Offer "design partner" pricing at $100K–$125K for 3 early adopters who agree to be references. This sacrifices margin for velocity.

**Month 3–4 (June–July 2026)**

6. **Max goes full-time and begins outreach** — Target 30–40 conversations, prioritizing companies where he has personal relationships
7. **Get 2–3 design partner commitments** — Even letters of intent or paid pilots at $25K–$50K demonstrate traction
8. **Conference presence** — Attend or sponsor a booth at a financial reporting conference (AICPA, FEI). The DISE topic is hot right now.
9. **Content marketing** — Publish 2–3 thought leadership pieces on DISE readiness. Max's KPMG pedigree makes these credible.

**Month 5–8 (August–November 2026)**

10. **Close design partners** — Convert pilots to full contracts
11. **Begin second wave of outreach** with case study evidence
12. **Hire a junior AE or SDR** to help Max with pipeline management
13. **Deliver exceptional service** to first clients — word-of-mouth in the CFO community is powerful

**Month 9 (December 2026)**

14. **Year-end contract push** — Companies with "use it or lose it" budgets may accelerate
15. **Secure pipeline for Q1 2027** — Many deals started in 2026 will close in early 2027

### Force Multipliers to Consider

1. **Channel partnerships** — Partner with a mid-tier consulting firm (Grant Thornton, BDO, RSM) who can resell your platform. This multiplies sales capacity instantly. These firms serve Fortune 500 companies and would love a tech-enabled offering to compete with Big 4 pricing.

2. **Target Fortune 501–1000 instead** — Mid-cap public companies have the same compliance mandates but faster procurement, smaller committees, and more price sensitivity. A company with $2B in revenue has the same DISE deadline as Apple but will close a deal in 8 weeks instead of 8 months.

3. **Start with one mandate, not three** — DISE is the hottest because the deadline is approaching and it's new. Focus your entire GTM on DISE and use the other two mandates as upsells after landing. Trying to sell three products from Day 1 dilutes your message.

4. **Land-and-expand pricing** — Sell DISE at $100K–$150K as an entry point, then upsell tax reconciliation and lease modules for another $100K–$150K each. Total account value reaches $300K+ but the initial procurement friction is lower.

## Part 5: Critical Risk Factors

### Risks That Could Kill This

| Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|------------|
| Max's non-compete blocks key contacts | Critical | Medium | Legal review before he leaves; target non-KPMG clients |
| Workiva launches a DISE module and bundles it free | High | High | Differentiate on implementation speed and cost |
| Fortune 500 companies decide to do DISE in-house | High | Medium | Target companies on legacy ERP (SAP ECC) where in-house is hardest |
| Data security incident or breach | Critical | Low | Invest in security from Day 1 |
| First client implementation fails | Critical | Low-Medium | Over-invest in first 2–3 clients |
| FASB delays DISE effective date | High | Low | Very unlikely given it's already finalized |
| Buyers wait until 2027 to purchase | High | Medium | Emphasize that readiness requires 6–12 months of prep |

### The Workiva Threat

Workiva ($885M revenue, heading to $1B+ in 2026) is the elephant in the room. They already serve a massive portion of the Fortune 500 for SEC reporting. Their likely move is to build DISE capabilities into their existing platform and offer it as an add-on to current clients. For companies already on Workiva, your pitch of "save money by using a startup" is a much harder sell than for companies not currently on Workiva.

**Your best targets are companies NOT on Workiva** who would otherwise hire KPMG/Deloitte/PwC for a one-time implementation project at $800K+.
